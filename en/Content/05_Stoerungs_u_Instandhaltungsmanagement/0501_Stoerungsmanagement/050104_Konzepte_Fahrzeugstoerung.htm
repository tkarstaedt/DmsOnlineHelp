      Fault Concept – Basics

      Fault management in PEAK.DMS records, categorizes, and evaluates vehicle defects using a structured system of fault levels, states, a vehicle fault tree, and capabilities.

      Overview

      The fault concept in PEAK.DMS comprises four central components:

      • Fault Levels (A–E): Assess the operational fault service level of the vehicle
      • Fault States: Control the processing workflow from reporting to resolution
      • Vehicle Fault Tree: Structures the categorization via Category / System / Component
      • Capabilities: Define negated equipment features and their effect on dispatching

      Together, these components form a complete system for recording, evaluating, and integrating vehicle faults into dispatching.

      Fault Levels

      The fault level assesses the operational fault service level of a vehicle. It controls the availability in dispatching and is crucial for automatic and manual block planning.

      Overview of Fault Levels

      PEAK.DMS distinguishes five fault levels from A (no restriction) to E (not serviceable):

      Level Designation Fault Service Level Effect on Dispatching
      A No restriction Fully serviceable Vehicle can be dispatched without restriction
      B Minor restriction Serviceable with minor restrictions Vehicle can be dispatched, possibly with a note to the driver
      C Limited service level Limited service level Vehicle can be dispatched with limitations (e.g., only for specific lines/blocks)
      D Conditionally serviceable Only usable in emergencies Vehicle blocked for automatic dispatching, manual assignment only in exceptional cases
      E Not serviceable Not serviceable Vehicle completely blocked, no dispatching possible

      Detailed Description of Fault Levels

      Fault Level A – No Restriction

      Fault Service Level: The vehicle is fully serviceable and can be dispatched without restrictions.

      Typical Faults:

      • Visual defects with no impact on operation (e.g., scratches, paint damage)
      • Minor soiling
      • Non-safety-relevant minor damages

      Effect on Dispatching: None. The vehicle is fully available.

      Fault Level B – Minor Restriction

      Fault Service Level: The vehicle is serviceable, but the fault should be rectified soon.

      Typical Faults:

      • Defective interior lighting (individual lights)
      • Failed passenger information (individual displays)
      • Minor ride comfort restrictions
      • Warning lights without immediate operational impact

      Effect on Dispatching: The vehicle is dispatched normally. Optionally, a note can be issued to the driver (project-specific).

      Fault Level C – Limited Service Level

      Fault Service Level: The vehicle is serviceable with restrictions. Operation may be limited to specific lines or blocks.

      Typical Faults:

      • Air conditioning failure (rated differently depending on the season and project)
      • Limited door function (e.g., one door failed)
      • Reduced seating capacity
      • Defective ramp/lift equipment (vehicle not accessible)
      • Defective passenger Wi-Fi

      Effect on Dispatching:

      • Vehicle is treated as a replacement vehicle type (e.g., "Bus without A/C")
      • Limited availability in automatic dispatching
      • Manual dispatching possible, considering the restriction
      • Negation of capabilities becomes effective (see Fault Concept – Basics)

      Fault Level D – Conditionally Serviceable

      Fault Service Level: The vehicle should only be used in emergencies and exceptional situations.

      Typical Faults:

      • Severely impaired vehicle safety (e.g., braking power reduced, but still sufficient)
      • Multiple failed doors
      • Significant chassis damage
      • Strong noise or odor nuisance
      • Defects requiring imminent decommissioning

      Effect on Dispatching:

      • Vehicle is completely blocked for automatic dispatching
      • Manual assignment only in justified exceptional cases by authorized dispatchers
      • Vehicle is displayed in vehicle list and dispatching overviews with a warning

      Fault Level E – Not Serviceable

      Fault Service Level: The vehicle is not serviceable and must not be used in passenger service.

      Typical Faults:

      • Safety-relevant damages (e.g., brake failure, steering problems)
      • Engine damage
      • Transmission damage
      • Missing operating permit
      • Accident damage
      • General inspection overdue

      Effect on Dispatching:

      • Vehicle is completely blocked for any type of dispatching
      • No automatic or manual assignment possible
      • Vehicle appears in dispatching views as "blocked" or "out of service"

      Aggregation Logic for Multiple Faults

      A vehicle can have multiple faults at the same time. In this case, the following logic applies:

      • Highest Fault Level Dominates: The vehicle's service level is determined by the most critical (highest) fault level.
      • Example: Vehicle has a Level B fault (defective interior lighting) and a Level D fault (limited brake function) → Vehicle is considered "conditionally serviceable" (Level D).
      • Display in Views: The Fault level column in the vehicle faults list shows the highest level for the vehicle.
      • Dispatching: Dispatching automatically uses the highest fault level to assess availability.

      Best Practices for Fault Level Selection

      Recommendation Reason
      Rate fault level conservatively When in doubt, choose a higher level to minimize risks.
      Consider season and operational profile A/C failure in mid-summer: Level C or D. In winter: possibly only Level B.
      Use vehicle fault tree The vehicle fault tree often contains suggestions for the fault level per component.
      Coordinate with dispatching Inform dispatching about critical faults (D/E) to compensate for downtimes.
      Regular review Re-evaluate fault levels for long-term faults regularly.

      Fault States and State Model

      Each vehicle fault goes through a defined lifecycle from creation to resolution or archiving. The state controls the visibility, editability, and dispatching effect of the fault.

      Description of Fault States

      State: Created (New)

      Meaning: The fault is currently being recorded in the dialog. The recording has not yet been completed with "OK".

      Visibility: Only visible to the recording user in the dialog. Does not appear in the fault list.

      Effect on Dispatching: None. The fault is not yet active.

      Possible Actions:

      • Complete recording → State changes to Reported
      • Cancel → Fault is discarded

      State: Reported

      Meaning: The fault has been recorded, saved, and is active. It is still unprocessed and awaiting action.

      Visibility: Visible in all fault views. Appears in vehicle dossier and possibly in driver information.

      Effect on Dispatching: Fault level is active. The fault affects the vehicle's availability in dispatching.

      Possible Actions:

      • Create garage order / Assign: → State changes to In Progress
      • Create vehicle reservation: → State changes to In Progress
      • Report finished / Completed: → State changes to Completed (for immediate resolution)
      • No Fault Found: → State changes to No Fault Found (if fault is not reproducible)

      Permissions: All authorized users (dispatching, depot, garage) can process reported faults.

      State: In Progress

      Meaning: The fault has been converted into a garage order or vehicle reservation and is being actively processed.

      Visibility: Still visible in fault views. Link to the garage order / vehicle reservation is displayed.

      Effect on Dispatching: Fault level is active. Vehicle may also be blocked by the garage order / vehicle reservation.

      Possible Actions:

      • Finish garage order: → Fault automatically changes to Completed
      • Finish vehicle reservation: → Fault automatically changes to Completed
      • Set to Completed manually: → State changes to Completed (for early resolution)
      • No Fault Found: → State changes to No Fault Found (if no defect is found after inspection)

      Permissions: Garage users and authorized dispatchers can change the state.

      State: Completed (Finished)

      Meaning: The fault was successfully resolved. The vehicle is fully serviceable again (regarding this fault).

      Visibility: Still appears in the fault list (until archiving). Can be hidden by filters.

      Effect on Dispatching: None. The fault level of this fault is no longer considered.

      Possible Actions:

      • View / Trace
      • After configured time: Automatic archiving

      Note: If there are multiple faults, the vehicle's fault level is calculated from the remaining uncompleted faults.

      State: No Fault Found

      Meaning: The fault was inspected but could not be reproduced. No defect was found.

      Visibility: Appears in the fault list marked as "No Fault Found".

      Effect on Dispatching: None. The fault level is no longer considered.

      Typical Scenarios:

      • Driver reports noise, garage cannot reproduce it
      • Warning light was active, does not appear during inspection
      • Error message could not be reproduced

      Possible Actions:

      • View / Trace
      • After configured time: Automatic archiving

      State Transitions

      From State To State Trigger Type
      Created Reported Complete dialog with "OK" Manual
      Created – Cancel dialog (fault is discarded) Manual
      Reported In Progress Create garage order / vehicle reservation Automatic
      Reported Completed Action "Report finished" / "Completed" Manual
      Reported No Fault Found Action "No Fault Found" Manual
      In Progress Completed Finish garage order / vehicle reservation Automatic
      In Progress Completed Action "Report finished" / "Completed" Manual
      In Progress No Fault Found Action "No Fault Found" after inspection Manual
      Completed – Archiving (after configured time) Automatic
      No Fault Found – Archiving (after configured time) Automatic

      Permissions for State Changes

      State Change Permission Required
      Create fault (Created → Reported) All users with fault recording permission
      Create garage order (Reported → In Progress) Dispatching, Garage
      Report fault as finished (→ Completed) Depending on project: All or only Garage/Dispatching
      Set to No Fault Found (→ No Fault Found) Garage, possibly restricted to garage management

      Note: Permissions are project-specifically configurable and can be adjusted by the administrator.

      Automatic vs. Manual State Changes

      Automatic State Changes:

      • Create garage order → In Progress
      • Finish garage order → Completed
      • Create vehicle reservation → In Progress
      • Finish vehicle reservation → Completed
      • Archiving after configured time

      Manual State Changes:

      • Complete dialog → Reported
      • Report finished → Completed
      • Set to No Fault Found → No Fault Found

      Vehicle Fault Tree and Fault Catalog

      The vehicle fault tree (also fault catalog) is a hierarchical structure for the systematic categorization of vehicle faults. It enables quick, structured recording and facilitates evaluations and statistics.

      Hierarchical Structure

      The vehicle fault tree has a three-level structure:

      1. Category (top level): Broad classification (e.g., "Powertrain", "Chassis", "Passenger Area", "Electrics")
      2. System (middle level): Functional systems within the category (e.g., "Engine", "Transmission", "Steering", "Air Conditioning")
      3. Component (lowest level): Specific parts or individual faults (e.g., "Spark plug", "Injection nozzle", "Door opener Door 1")

      Example: Vehicle Fault Tree Path

      			Category:     Passenger Area
			System:       Air Conditioning
			Component:    A/C compressor defective
			Component:    Ventilation failed
			Component:    Heating defective
			System:       Doors
			Component:    Door opener Door 1
			Component:    Door closer Door 2
			Component:    Door sensor defective

      Vehicle Type-Specific Configuration

      The vehicle fault tree is configured per vehicle type. This means:

      • Different vehicle types can have different vehicle fault trees
      • When recording a fault, the vehicle fault tree matching the vehicle type is loaded automatically
      • Example: An articulated bus has different components than a standard bus (e.g., additional doors, articulation joint)

      Note: The vehicle fault tree configuration is done by the administrator via the system configuration.

      Fault Numbers and Quick Entry

      Each entry in the vehicle fault tree can be assigned a fault number. This allows for accelerated recording:

      • Enter the fault number in the "Fault No." or "Quick Entry" field
      • System automatically navigates to the corresponding entry in the vehicle fault tree
      • All stored information (fault level, text, etc.) is adopted

      Use Scenario: Garage staff often know common fault numbers by heart and can enter them directly without navigating through the tree.

      Stored Information in the Vehicle Fault Tree

      The following information can be stored for each entry (especially at the component level):

      Information Description Usage
      Fault Level (Suggestion) Recommended fault level for this fault (A–E) Is automatically suggested upon selection, can be overridden by the user
      Fault Text Standardized description of the fault Is copied to the "Description" field, can be supplemented
      Action Note Notes for garage or driver (e.g., "Spare part in stock") Is displayed in the dialog and copied to the garage order
      Capability (negated) Equipment feature that fails due to the fault Controls calculation of the dispatch vehicle type (see Fault Concept – Basics)
      Documents / Images References to repair manuals, circuit diagrams, photos Are linked in the dialog and can be opened

      Administration and Customization

      The vehicle fault tree is maintained via the System Configuration:

      • Access: Only administrators or authorized configuration users
      • Menu: Configuration → Master Data → Vehicle Fault Tree (project-specific)
      • Functions: Add, edit, delete categories / systems / components; assign fault numbers; associate capabilities

      Best Practice: Changes to the vehicle fault tree should be documented and coordinated with the garage and dispatching.

      Document Linking

      Documents can be linked to vehicle fault tree entries:

      • Repair manuals (PDF)
      • Circuit diagrams, technical drawings
      • Photos of typical damages
      • Checklists for inspection and resolution

      These documents are displayed in the Vehicle Fault dialog and can be opened directly.

      Capabilities and their Negation

      Capabilities describe the equipment of a vehicle (e.g., air conditioning, low-floor technology, Wi-Fi). In case of faults, capabilities can be negated to reflect the reduced service level.

      What are Capabilities?

      Capabilities are configurable attributes that describe the functional equipment of a vehicle. They are used to:

      • Filter and search for vehicles by equipment
      • Differentiate vehicle types (e.g., "Standard Bus" vs. "Standard Bus with A/C")
      • Meet dispatching requirements (e.g., "Block requires vehicle with low-floor")

      Typical Capabilities

      Capability Description
      Air Conditioning Vehicle has functioning air conditioning
      Low-Floor Vehicle is low-floor and accessible
      Ramp / Lift Equipment Vehicle has a ramp or lift for wheelchair users
      Wi-Fi Passenger Wi-Fi available
      USB Charging Stations Charging options for passengers available
      Video Surveillance Camera surveillance active
      Passenger Information Dynamic passenger information (displays, announcements)

      Negated Capabilities in Faults

      If a fault impairs a capability, it can be negated:

      • In the vehicle fault tree, a capability is associated with the fault entry
      • When recording a fault with this entry, the capability is automatically negated for this vehicle
      • The vehicle is now considered "without this capability"

      Example: Air Conditioning Defective

      1. Fault is recorded: "A/C compressor defective" (from vehicle fault tree)
      2. In the vehicle fault tree, the capability "Air Conditioning" is stored for this entry
      3. When saving the fault, the capability "Air Conditioning" is negated for the vehicle
      4. The vehicle is now considered a "Bus without A/C"

      Effect on Dispatch Vehicle Type

      The negation of capabilities leads to the calculation of a dispatch vehicle type (also replacement vehicle type):

      • Original vehicle type: "Standard Bus with A/C"
      • Negated capability: "Air Conditioning"
      • Calculated dispatch vehicle type: "Standard Bus without A/C"

      Dispatching uses the dispatch vehicle type for availability calculation and assignment:

      • Blocks that explicitly require "Vehicle with A/C" cannot be assigned to this vehicle
      • Blocks without this requirement can still use the vehicle

      Examples of Capability Negation

      Fault Negated Capability Effect
      Air conditioning failed Air Conditioning Vehicle not available for blocks requiring air conditioning
      Ramp defective Accessibility / Ramp Vehicle blocked for accessible blocks
      Wi-Fi router failed Wi-Fi Vehicle not available for lines requiring Wi-Fi (project-specific)
      Door 3 defective Full door function Vehicle only usable for lines with low passenger frequency
      Video surveillance defective Video Surveillance Vehicle possibly blocked for night lines (project-specific)

      Multiple Negations

      A vehicle can have multiple faults with different negated capabilities:

      • Example: Fault 1 negates "Air Conditioning", Fault 2 negates "Wi-Fi"
      • Result: Vehicle is considered "without A/C" AND "without Wi-Fi"
      • Dispatch vehicle type: "Standard Bus without A/C and Wi-Fi"

      Restoration of Capabilities

      As soon as a fault is set to Completed or No Fault Found:

      • The negated capability is automatically restored
      • The dispatch vehicle type is recalculated
      • The vehicle is available again with its full range of capabilities (provided no other faults are present)

      Configuration of Capabilities

      Capabilities are configured by the administrator:

      • Definition: What capabilities exist in the system?
      • Association with Vehicle Types: Which vehicle types have which capabilities by default?
      • Association in Vehicle Fault Tree: Which fault negates which capability?
      • Dispatching Rules: Which blocks/lines require which capabilities?

      Best Practices – Overall Overview

      Fault Recording

      • Use vehicle fault tree completely: Navigate to the component level for precise categorization
      • Choose fault level carefully: Consider dispatching impacts
      • Complete description: Record symptoms, action notes, and remarks
      • Check capabilities: Verify if negated capabilities are correct

      Fault Processing

      • Report quick faults as finished directly: Avoid unnecessary garage orders
      • Bundle multiple faults: Use "Add all open faults" when creating a garage order
      • Document "No Fault Found": Record why no fault was found
      • Keep state up-to-date: Avoid outdated states

      Dispatching

      • Monitor fault levels: Regularly check vehicles with Level C/D/E
      • Consider dispatch vehicle type: Pay attention to negated capabilities during block assignment
      • Communicate with garage: Coordinate critical faults

      Administration

      • Keep vehicle fault tree up-to-date: Adapt categories to new vehicle types
      • Define capabilities consistently: Avoid contradictions in dispatching rules
      • Check permissions regularly: Ensure only authorized users perform critical actions
      • Optimize archiving times: Balance between traceability and performance
